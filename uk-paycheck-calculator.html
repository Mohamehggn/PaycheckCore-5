Complete Guide to UK Tax, National Insurance & Take-Home Pay 2025/26

Understanding how your salary is taxed in the UK is essential for financial planning. The 2025/26 tax year runs from 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2026. Your take-home pay is your gross salary minus deductions for Income Tax, National Insurance, pension contributions, and student loan repayments. This guide explains every aspect of the UK PAYE system for employees in England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.

UK Income Tax Bands 2025/26 Explained

Income Tax is calculated using a progressive system where different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. The UK government sets these tax bands annually. For the 2025/26 tax year, the Personal Allowance remains frozen at £12,570. This means you can earn up to £12,570 before paying any Income Tax. For every £2 you earn over £100,000, your Personal Allowance reduces by £1. This means your Personal Allowance is zero once your income reaches £125,140.

If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, your Income Tax is calculated using these rates for 2025/26:

Example: If you earn £60,000 in England, you pay 0% on the first £12,570, 20% on the next £37,700 (up to £50,270), and 40% on the final £9,730. Your total Income Tax would be £7,540 + £3,892 = £11,432 for the year.

Scottish Income Tax Bands 2025/26

The Scottish Parliament sets different Income Tax rates. If you are a Scottish taxpayer, you use these six bands instead of the UK rates. Your Scottish taxpayer status is based on your main residence, not where you work. The Personal Allowance of £12,570 still applies across the UK.

National Insurance Contributions 2025/26

National Insurance (NI) is a tax on earnings that qualifies you for certain state benefits, including the State Pension. Employees pay Class 1 NI through PAYE. For 2025/26, the NI thresholds align with the Income Tax Personal Allowance. You pay NI on earnings above £12,570 per year, which equals £242 per week or £1,048 per month.

  • 0% on earnings up to £12,570: No NI due
  • 8% on earnings from £12,571 to £50,270: This is called the Primary Threshold to Upper Earnings Limit
  • 2% on earnings over £50,270: Earnings above the Upper Earnings Limit

Example: On a £40,000 salary, you pay 8% NI on £27,430 (£40,000 - £12,570), which equals £2,194.40 per year or £182.87 per month. If you earned £60,000, you would pay 8% on £37,700 = £3,016, plus 2% on £9,730 = £194.60. Total NI = £3,210.60 annually. Unlike Income Tax, NI rates are the same across the entire UK, including Scotland.

Understanding Your Tax Code

Your tax code is issued by HMRC and tells your employer how much Income Tax to deduct from your pay. It is usually found on your payslip, P45, P60, or PAYE coding notice. The numbers in your tax code represent your Personal Allowance divided by 10. Letters show how it should be adjusted.

Common Tax Codes for 2025/26:

  • 1257L: The standard code for those with one job and the full £12,570 Personal Allowance.
  • BR: Basic Rate. All income taxed at 20%. Used for second jobs or pensions.
  • D0: Higher Rate. All income taxed at 40%. Used for additional jobs when you are already a higher rate taxpayer.
  • D1: Additional Rate. All income taxed at 45%.
  • S1257L: Scottish standard code. The 'S' prefix means Scottish rates apply.
  • K: You have income not taxed elsewhere, like company benefits, that exceeds your Personal Allowance. Additional tax is collected via PAYE.
  • NT: No tax deducted. Used in specific circumstances.

If you think your tax code is wrong, contact HMRC. An incorrect code means you could pay too much or too little tax. HMRC will issue an updated code to your employer. Tax codes can change mid-year if your circumstances change, such as receiving new benefits-in-kind or starting a second job.

Workplace Pension Contributions

Under auto-enrolment, most UK employees are automatically enrolled in a workplace pension. The minimum total contribution is 8% of qualifying earnings, made up of 5% from you and 3% from your employer. Qualifying earnings for 2025/26 are between £6,240 and £50,270. Many employers and employees contribute more than the minimum.

Pension contributions are commonly taken via salary sacrifice or a net pay arrangement. Both methods provide tax relief at your marginal rate. With salary sacrifice, your gross pay is reduced before tax and NI are calculated, which usually means you save on National Insurance too. For example, a 5% pension contribution on £40,000 saves you £400 in Income Tax (20%) and £160 in NI (8%), so the net cost of a £2,000 pension contribution is only £1,440. Our calculator assumes a salary sacrifice or net pay arrangement.

Student Loan Repayments 2025/26

If you have a student loan, repayments are deducted through PAYE when your income exceeds the threshold for your plan. You only make repayments on income above the threshold, and only at 9% of that amount. Postgraduate Loans are repaid at 6%. You stop making repayments if your income drops below the threshold or when the loan is paid off.

High Income Child Benefit Charge & Personal Allowance Taper

Two additional factors can affect high earners. First, the Personal Allowance taper: For every £2 earned above £100,000, you lose £1 of your Personal Allowance. This creates an effective 60% marginal tax rate between £100,000 and £125,140. Once you reach £125,140, your Personal Allowance is zero. Second, if you or your partner claim Child Benefit and one of you earns over £60,000, you may have to pay the High Income Child Benefit Charge. The charge is 1% of the benefit for every £200 earned over £60,000. Once you reach £80,000, the charge equals the entire benefit.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my take-home pay lower than I expected?

The two largest deductions are Income Tax and National Insurance. On a £40,000 salary, these total £5,492 and £1,460 respectively. Adding pension and student loan contributions further reduces net pay. Remember that your employer also pays Employer's National Insurance on your salary, but this does not affect your take-home pay.

Do I pay tax on bonuses and overtime?

Yes. Bonuses, commission, and overtime are treated as regular earnings and taxed through PAYE. HMRC may use an 'emergency tax code' like 1257L W1/M1 for one-off payments, which can temporarily over-tax you. Any overpayment is usually refunded automatically or can be reclaimed.

How is Scottish tax different from the rest of the UK?

Scotland has six Income Tax bands compared to three in rUK. Scottish rates start lower at 19% but rise to 48% for top earners, compared to 45% in England. At £50,000, a Scottish taxpayer pays £1,706 more Income Tax than someone in England. However, tax codes still provide a £12,570 Personal Allowance UK-wide.

Should I opt out of my workplace pension to increase take-home pay?

While opting out increases your immediate net pay, you lose employer contributions and tax relief. Employer contributions are effectively free money. A 5% employee contribution with 3% employer match means you get a 60% return before any investment growth. For most people, staying enrolled is beneficial for long-term retirement planning.

When do student loan repayments stop?

Plan 1 loans are written off after 25 years or when you reach 65. Plan 2 loans are written off 30 years after you become eligible to repay. Plan 4 is 30 years. Plan 5 is 40 years. Postgraduate loans are written off after 30 years. Any remaining balance is cancelled. You can make voluntary additional payments to clear your loan faster.
