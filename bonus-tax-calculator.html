Bonus Tax Calculator: How Much Tax Is Taken Out of a Bonus in 2026?

Getting a bonus feels great until you see your paycheck and wonder where all the money went. If you're asking "how are bonuses taxed" or "why is my bonus taxed so high," you're not alone. Bonus pay is considered supplemental wages by the IRS, and that means it can be taxed differently than your regular salary. Our bonus tax calculator shows you exactly how much federal tax, state tax, FICA, and Medicare will be withheld from your 2026 bonus.

Understanding Supplemental Wage Taxation

The IRS classifies bonuses, commissions, overtime pay, severance, back pay, and other non-regular wages as "supplemental wages." Employers have two IRS-approved methods to withhold federal income tax on supplemental wages: the percentage method also called the flat rate method, and the aggregate method. Most employers default to the 22% flat rate method because it's administratively easier, but the method they use significantly impacts your take-home bonus amount.

For 2026, the IRS supplemental tax rate remains 22% for bonuses up to $1 million. If your bonus exceeds $1 million in a calendar year, the excess is subject to a 37% mandatory flat rate withholding. This doesn't mean you're taxed at 22% or 37% overall. It only affects withholding. Your actual tax liability is determined when you file your annual tax return based on your total income and tax bracket.

Flat Rate Method vs Aggregate Method Explained

22% Flat Rate Method: This is the most common approach for bonus paycheck calculator scenarios. Your employer withholds exactly 22% for federal income tax regardless of your tax bracket. So a $10,000 bonus has $2,200 withheld federally. Add Social Security at 6.2%, Medicare at 1.45%, and your state tax rate, and you easily see 30-40% total withholding. The advantage is predictability. The disadvantage is that if you're in a 12% or 24% tax bracket, you might over-withhold and get a larger refund, or under-withhold if you're in the 32%+ brackets.

Aggregate Method: Your employer adds your bonus to your most recent regular paycheck and calculates withholding as if the total were a single payment for the period. This method looks at your W-4 settings and annualizes the combined amount. If your regular biweekly paycheck is $3,000 and you get a $5,000 bonus, withholding is calculated on $8,000 as if you make that every pay period. This often results in higher withholding than 22% because it pushes you into higher tax brackets for that calculation. Many employees prefer the flat rate because aggregate can withhold 30%+ for federal alone.

FICA Taxes on Bonuses: Social Security and Medicare

Beyond federal income tax, your bonus is subject to FICA taxes exactly like regular wages. For 2026, Social Security tax is 6.2% on earnings up to the wage base limit of $176,100. Medicare tax is 1.45% on all earnings with no cap. If you earn over $200,000 as a single filer or $250,000 married filing jointly, you pay an Additional Medicare Tax of 0.9% on wages above those thresholds. These FICA amounts are mandatory and cannot be adjusted on your W-4. A tax on bonus calculator must include these to be accurate.

State Tax on Bonus Pay

State taxation of bonuses varies dramatically. Seven states have no income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Your bonus receives no state withholding in these states. Other states either mandate a flat supplemental wage rate or require aggregate withholding. For example, California requires 10.23% on bonuses over certain amounts and 6.6% otherwise. New York withholds 9.62% on supplemental wages. Pennsylvania uses a flat 3.07%. You can find your state's rate in our bonus tax calculator dropdown. Note that Tennessee and New Hampshire only tax dividend and interest income, not wages, so bonuses there have no state withholding.

How to Calculate Tax on Bonus: Step-by-Step Example

Let's calculate tax on a $7,500 bonus for someone earning $85,000 annually in Ohio, paid biweekly, filing single:

  1. Federal Flat Rate: $7,500 × 22% = $1,650 federal income tax withheld
  2. State Tax: Ohio supplemental rate 3.5%, so $7,500 × 3.5% = $262.50
  3. Social Security: $7,500 × 6.2% = $465
  4. Medicare: $7,500 × 1.45% = $108.75
  5. Total Withholding: $1,650 + $262.50 + $465 + $108.75 = $2,486.25
  6. Net Bonus: $7,500 - $2,486.25 = $5,013.75 received
  7. Effective Rate: $2,486.25 / $7,500 = 33.15% total tax rate on bonus

If this same bonus used aggregate method with biweekly salary of $3,269, the system would calculate withholding on $10,769 as if annualized to $280,000. That pushes the federal withholding into the 24-32% bracket ranges, likely taking $2,000+ federal alone. This is why people search "why was my bonus taxed 40%" after seeing aggregate withholding.

Can You Avoid High Tax on Bonuses?

You cannot legally avoid FICA taxes or change the IRS supplemental wage rules. However, you have strategies to manage the tax impact. First, you can adjust your W-4 to have less withheld from regular paychecks to offset bonus over-withholding, but this risks under-withholding if you miscalculate. Second, increase 401k contributions from your bonus. Traditional 401k deferrals reduce your taxable wages dollar-for-dollar. A $5,000 bonus with $5,000 401k contribution results in $0 federal and state income tax withholding, though FICA still applies. Third, request your employer pay the bonus in January instead of December if you're close to Social Security wage base limits or expect lower income next year.

Bonus vs Regular Pay: What's the Tax Difference?

Bonuses aren't taxed at a higher rate. They're withheld at a higher rate. This distinction matters. Your regular pay uses tax tables that account for your standard deduction, filing status, and tax brackets across the full year. The 22% flat rate assumes you're roughly in the 22% bracket. If your actual marginal rate is 12%, you'll get a refund of the extra 10% withheld. If you're in the 32% bracket, you might owe more at tax time. The IRS views all income the same on your 1040. The withholding method is just an estimate. Many people searching "bonus paycheck calculator" are shocked by the withholding, but annual tax reconciliation balances it out.

Frequently Asked Questions About Bonus Taxes

Why is my bonus taxed at 40% or higher? ▼
Your bonus isn't taxed at 40%, it's withheld at that rate. The 22% federal flat rate plus 6.2% Social Security, 1.45% Medicare, and state tax of 3-10% easily totals 35-42%. If your employer uses aggregate method, federal withholding can exceed 30% alone. You get any excess back as a tax refund when you file. The withholding rate feels high because it doesn't account for deductions and applies a flat percentage to the entire bonus amount.
What is the 2026 federal tax rate on bonuses? ▼
For 2026, the IRS supplemental wage withholding rate is 22% for bonuses up to $1 million. If your cumulative bonuses exceed $1 million in the calendar year, the excess is subject to 37% mandatory withholding. This is only withholding, not your final tax rate. Your actual tax rate depends on your total annual income and tax bracket when you file your return.
Do I pay Social Security and Medicare tax on my bonus? ▼
Yes. Bonuses are subject to FICA taxes exactly like regular wages. You pay 6.2% Social Security tax on wages up to $176,100 for 2026, and 1.45% Medicare tax on all wages with no limit. If you earn over $200,000 single or $250,000 married filing jointly, an Additional Medicare Tax of 0.9% applies to wages above those thresholds. You cannot exempt bonuses from FICA.
Which states don't tax bonuses? ▼
Seven states have no state income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Tennessee and New Hampshire don't tax wage income, only dividends and interest. If you live in these states, your bonus will have zero state tax withheld. All other states tax bonuses either at a flat supplemental rate or via aggregate withholding based on your W-4. Rates range from 1.84% in North Dakota to 9.62% in New York.
Can my employer use a different tax rate for my bonus? ▼
Employers must use one of two IRS-approved methods: the 22% flat rate method or the aggregate method. They cannot pick arbitrary percentages. Most companies use 22% for bonuses under $1 million because it's simpler. If you receive multiple bonuses and your employer uses aggregate method, each bonus could have different withholding based on timing and your regular pay. You cannot request a specific rate, but you can adjust your W-4 to change total annual withholding.
How can I reduce taxes on my bonus? ▼
You cannot reduce the mandatory withholding percentages, but you can reduce taxable income. Contribute your bonus to a traditional 401k or 403b, which avoids federal and state income tax withholding on that amount. FICA still applies. You can also contribute to an HSA if eligible. Another strategy is timing: if you're near the Social Security wage base, deferring a December bonus to January might eliminate the 6.2% SS tax. Finally, adjust your W-4 mid-year to account for bonus over-withholding and increase take-home pay on regular checks.
Is it better to get a bonus in December or January? ▼
It depends on your situation. December bonuses count toward current year income and might push you into a higher tax bracket or trigger phase-outs of deductions and credits. If you've already exceeded the Social Security wage base of $176,100 for 2026, a December bonus avoids the 6.2% SS tax. January bonuses defer taxation to next year and restart your FICA calculations. If you expect lower income next year, January is usually better. Consult a tax professional for your specific case.
What's the difference between flat rate and aggregate method? ▼
The flat rate method withholds exactly 22% federal tax regardless of your salary or tax bracket. The aggregate method combines your bonus with your last regular paycheck and calculates withholding as if that combined amount is your normal pay, using tax tables and your W-4. Aggregate often withholds more because it assumes the higher amount year-round, pushing you into higher brackets. Flat rate is predictable at 22%. Most employers choose flat rate for simplicity unless payroll systems default to aggregate.
Will I get the extra tax back on my bonus? ▼
Yes, if too much was withheld. When you file your tax return, the IRS looks at total income and total withholding from all sources. If your bonus was withheld at 22% but your actual marginal rate is 12%, you get the 10% difference back as a refund. If you're in the 32% bracket, you might owe more. The bonus tax calculator shows withholding, not final liability. Many people receive larger refunds specifically because of bonus over-withholding.
Are commissions taxed like bonuses? ▼
Yes. The IRS classifies commissions as supplemental wages subject to the same rules as bonuses. Employers can use either the 22% flat rate or aggregate method. If commissions are paid separately from regular wages, they follow supplemental wage rules. If commissions are combined with regular pay in the same paycheck without being separately identified, they're taxed as regular wages using tax tables. FICA and state taxes apply identically to bonuses and commissions.
