Understanding Canadian Payroll Deductions 2026

Canada uses a progressive tax system where both federal and provincial governments levy income tax. In addition to taxes, most employees pay into the Canada Pension Plan (CPP) or Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) and Employment Insurance (EI) or the Quebec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP). Understanding these deductions is critical for financial planning, budgeting, and comparing job offers across provinces.

Federal Income Tax Brackets 2026

The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) adjusts federal tax brackets annually for inflation using the indexation factor. For 2026, the projected federal brackets are based on a 2.6% indexation from 2025 rates. Note that official 2026 rates are typically confirmed by the CRA in late 2025. The brackets below use CRA's standard indexation methodology and reflect the latest published projections.

All Canadian residents claim the Basic Personal Amount (BPA). For 2026, the maximum BPA is projected at $16,129. This non-refundable credit means you pay no federal tax on the first $16,129 of income. The BPA is gradually reduced for individuals with net income above $177,890 and fully eliminated at $253,414.

Provincial and Territorial Income Tax 2026

Each province and territory sets its own tax rates and brackets. This creates significant differences in net pay across Canada. Alberta currently has no provincial sales tax and uses a single tax rate system after a basic exemption, while Quebec has the highest overall income tax burden but provides extensive public services. Here are the projected 2026 tax brackets for all 13 jurisdictions:

Ontario (ON)

Ontario applies a 5.05% rate on the first $51,446, then 9.15% to $102,894, 11.16% to $150,000, 12.16% to $220,000, and 13.16% above. Ontario also levies a surtax on high-income earners and has an Ontario Health Premium up to $900.

Quebec (QC)

Quebec uses 14% to $53,359, 19% to $106,718, 24% to $129,978, and 25.75% above. Quebec residents do not pay into CPP or EI. Instead, they contribute to QPP (6.4% in 2026) and QPIP (0.494%). Quebec tax credits generally replace federal amounts, and the Basic Personal Amount is $18,056 for 2026.

British Columbia (BC)

BC rates: 5.06% to $47,933, 7.7% to $95,866, 10.5% to $109,999, 12.29% to $133,175, 14.7% to $181,232, 16.8% to $247,752, and 20.5% above. The BC climate action tax credit provides relief for low-income families.

Alberta (AB)

Alberta taxes 10% on the first $146,260, then 12% to $175,511, 13% to $210,617, 14% to $334,214, and 15% above. With no provincial sales tax and low income tax, Alberta often provides the highest take-home pay in Canada for middle and high earners.

Manitoba (MB)

Manitoba: 10.8% to $39,794, 12.75% to $77,926, and 17.4% above. The basic personal amount for 2026 is $15,725.

Saskatchewan (SK)

Saskatchewan: 10.5% to $49,720, 12.5% to $142,058, and 14.5% above. Saskatchewan indexes its brackets and basic personal amount of $18,078 to inflation.

Nova Scotia (NS)

Nova Scotia: 8.79% to $31,368, 14.95% to $62,737, 16.67% to $95,361, 17.5% to $115,481, and 21% above. NS has among the highest top marginal rates in Canada.

New Brunswick (NB)

New Brunswick: 9.4% to $50,673, 14.0% to $101,347, 16.0% to $185,001, and 19.5% above.

Prince Edward Island (PE)

PEI: 9.65% to $32,656, 13.63% to $64,313, 16.65% to $105,000, and 18.75% above.

Newfoundland and Labrador (NL)

NL: 8.7% to $43,636, 14.5% to $87,272, 15.8% to $155,880, 18.3% to $217,930, 19.8% to $278,590, 21.3% to $557,191, 21.8% to $1,114,385, and 22.1% above.

Yukon (YT)

Yukon mirrors federal brackets but applies rates of 6.4%, 9.0%, 10.9%, 12.8%, and 15%. Residents also receive the Yukon Child Benefit and carbon rebate.

Northwest Territories (NT)

NT: 5.9% to $52,981, 8.6% to $105,969, 12.2% to $171,803, and 14.05% above. The cost of living tax credit helps offset high northern expenses.

Nunavut (NU)

Nunavut: 4.0% to $55,384, 7.0% to $110,769, 9.0% to $180,350, and 11.5% above. Nunavut has the lowest tax rates in Canada but the highest cost of living.

Important: Tax rates and brackets for 2026 are projections based on historical indexation. The CRA and provincial ministries confirm final numbers each fall. Always verify with your employer or a qualified tax professional for precise payroll calculations.

Canada Pension Plan (CPP) and Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) 2026

The CPP is a mandatory contributory social insurance program. For 2026, employees and employers each contribute 5.95% of pensionable earnings. The Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YMPE) is $73,200, with a basic exemption of $3,500. This means the maximum annual CPP contribution is $4,150.65 calculated as ($73,200 - $3,500) x 5.95%.

Quebec opted out of CPP and runs QPP. The 2026 QPP rate is 6.4% per employee and employer, reflecting Quebec's enhanced benefit structure. Maximum QPP for 2026 is approximately $4,460.80. Both CPP and QPP now include an enhanced portion introduced in 2019 that gradually increased contribution rates to provide higher retirement benefits.

Self-employed individuals pay both employee and employer portions: 11.9% for CPP or 12.8% for QPP in 2026. You only pay CPP/QPP on employment income between $3,500 and the YMPE. Income below $3,500 or above $73,200 is not subject to CPP.

Employment Insurance (EI) and Quebec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP) 2026

EI provides temporary financial assistance to unemployed Canadians while they look for work or upgrade skills. The 2026 EI premium rate is 1.63% for employees outside Quebec, up to the Maximum Insurable Earnings (MIE) of $65,700. Maximum annual EI is $1,071.11.

Quebec residents pay a reduced EI rate of 1.27% because the province administers its own parental benefits through QPIP. QPIP rates for 2026 are 0.494% for employees on insurable earnings up to $98,000. This supports Quebec's 55-week parental leave system, which is more generous than the rest of Canada.

Employers pay 1.4 times the employee EI premium. If you earn more than the MIE, no additional EI is deducted from your paycheque for the remainder of the year.

RRSP Contributions and Tax Savings

A Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) lets you defer tax on income you save for retirement. Contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar, which can lower both federal and provincial tax. For 2026, the RRSP deduction limit is 18% of your 2025 earned income up to $32,490, plus any unused contribution room carried forward.

Example: If you earn $80,000 and contribute $10,000 to your RRSP, your taxable income falls to $70,000. At a 30% marginal rate, you save $3,000 in tax that year. The calculator above applies your RRSP input before computing federal and provincial tax. Note that CPP, EI, QPP, and QPIP are calculated on gross earnings before RRSP deductions.

Maximizing RRSP contributions before March 1, 2027 allows you to claim the deduction on your 2026 tax return. However, consider your TFSA first if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket during retirement.

How Province Selection Impacts Take-Home Pay

Moving between provinces can change your net pay by thousands of dollars annually. A $100,000 salary yields approximately:

  • Alberta: $72,100 net pay - lowest provincial tax, no PST
  • Ontario: $70,800 net pay - moderate tax, health premium applies
  • British Columbia: $71,500 net pay - lower rates at middle income
  • Quebec: $67,200 net pay - highest deductions but includes QPIP, QPP, and subsidized daycare
  • Nova Scotia: $68,900 net pay - higher top brackets

These figures assume no RRSP and standard credits only. Quebec's lower net is offset by public services including $8.70/day childcare, pharmacare, and extended parental leave. Always compare cost of living, housing, and services, not just income tax.

Additional Payroll Deductions Not Included

This calculator focuses on mandatory statutory deductions. Your actual paycheque may also include:

  • Union dues: Mandatory if you work in a unionized environment.
  • Benefits premiums: Extended health, dental, disability insurance.
  • Pension plan contributions: Employer RPP or group RRSP beyond CPP.
  • Court-ordered deductions: Child support, garnishments.
  • Charitable donations: Through payroll giving programs.

Speak with your HR department for a complete breakdown. For self-employed individuals, you must remit CPP and EI yourself and make quarterly tax instalments.

Tax Planning Tips for 2026

  1. Maximize tax credits: Claim tuition, medical expenses, donation credits, and the Canada Workers Benefit if eligible.
  2. Income splitting: Pension income splitting with a spouse age 65+ can save thousands.
  3. Time your bonuses: If you expect lower income next year, ask to defer a year-end bonus to January.
  4. Use a TFSA: For investments when your current tax rate is lower than your expected retirement rate.
  5. Track work-from-home expenses: The CRA simplified method allows $2/day up to $500 without receipts.

Frequently Asked Questions

When are 2026 tax rates officially confirmed?

The CRA announces indexed amounts each November. Provincial budgets from February to April confirm 2026 brackets. This calculator uses CRA's indexation formula and will be updated once official rates release.

Why is my actual paycheque different?

Employers may adjust tax withholding based on TD1 forms, benefits, bonuses, or retroactive pay. The calculator assumes consistent pay periods and basic credits only. Year-to-date amounts also affect CPP/EI once you hit the maximum.

Do I pay CPP and EI on bonus income?

Yes. Bonuses, commissions, and vacation pay are insurable and pensionable earnings. They are subject to CPP, EI, and tax, though employers often withhold tax at a higher lump-sum rate.

What is the difference between gross and taxable income?

Gross income is your total earnings. Taxable income is gross income minus deductions like RRSP contributions, union dues, and employment expenses. Tax and credits are calculated on taxable income.
