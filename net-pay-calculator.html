How to Use the Net Pay Calculator

This net pay calculator shows exactly how much money hits your bank account after all taxes and deductions. Use it to budget accurately and understand your true take-home income.

Step 1: Enter Gross Wages

Input your gross pay per paycheck before any deductions. Find this on your pay stub or offer letter.

Step 2: Input Tax Rates

Enter your federal and state income tax percentages. If unsure, use 12% federal and 5% state as averages, or check your last pay stub. FICA is always 7.65% for most W-2 employees.

Step 3: Add Pre-Tax Deductions

Include 401k %, health insurance cost, HSA/FSA contributions, and other pre-tax benefits. These reduce your taxable income and net pay.

What is Net Pay vs Gross Pay?

  • Gross Pay: Total earnings before any deductions - your stated salary or hourly wage
  • Net Pay: Actual take-home pay deposited in your bank after taxes and deductions
  • Difference: Average 20-30% of gross pay goes to taxes and benefits

Common Net Pay Scenarios 2026

  • $3,000 gross biweekly = ~$2,100-$2,300 net pay (single, standard deductions)
  • $5,000 gross monthly = ~$3,500-$3,800 net pay (married, 401k + health)
  • $4,000 gross biweekly = ~$2,800-$3,100 net pay (single, high tax state)
  • $2,500 gross weekly = ~$1,750-$1,950 net pay (standard deductions)

Why Your Net Pay Matters

Your net pay determines your actual budget for rent, bills, savings, and spending. Many people budget based on gross salary and run short. Always budget using net pay - the money you actually receive.
